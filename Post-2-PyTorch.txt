We talked about the math behind State Space Models (SSMs) and Mamba in Part 1. Now it’s time to get our hands dirty and actually build one. 🛠️

If you’ve ever looked at the original S4 or Mamba codebases, they can feel incredibly intimidating—packed with complex CUDA kernels and hardware-aware parallel scans. 

But at its mathematical core, the logic of a 1-Dimensional State Space Model is beautifully simple. 

For Part 2 of my "Build in Public" series, I stripped away all the hardcore engineering optimizations to write a minimal, educational 1D State Space layer in under 100 lines of pure PyTorch. 🐍

Here is the recipe:
1️⃣ Discretization: Converting our continuous-time matrices (A, B) into discrete steps using the Zero-Order Hold (ZOH) technique.
2️⃣ The Recurrent View: A simple step-by-step update of the hidden state as new tokens arrive (perfect for $O(1)$ fast inference).
3️⃣ The Convolutional View: Unrolling the linear system into a massive 1D global convolution kernel to process the entire sequence simultaneously (perfect for parallel training).

When you see it written in pure PyTorch, the famous duality of SSMs clicks. You literally define your continuous representations as `nn.Parameter`s, calculate the discretized `K` kernel, and apply `F.conv1d`. That's it!

💡 But here is where Control Theory math meets engineering reality: My first implementation failed spectacularly!
1. Exploding Exponentials: Initializing the state matrix A randomly caused the exponential ZOH mappings to blow up to infinity. A MUST be strictly negative to force healthy decay!
2. The PyTorch Gotcha: `F.conv1d` computes cross-correlation, not convolution! You must physically `torch.flip()` your response kernel, otherwise future tokens bleed backwards in time!
